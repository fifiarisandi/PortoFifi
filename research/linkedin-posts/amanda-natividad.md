"What's our AI visibility like?"
If my CEO or CMO asked me that, here's what I'd do.

First, run our brand through HubSpot's AEO Grader. Then summarize what stands out.

I did this for SparkToro.

The report correctly identified us as a niche audience research tool. And it picked up on one of our biggest advantages: credibility with marketing practitioners.

That's affirming because it confirms what I already suspected.

Other parts need a human read:

The competitive set is messy. SparkToro gets compared to Semrush, Ahrefs, Similarweb, Brandwatch, and more. Some are fair nearby alternatives. They're not all direct competitors. But this info is useful — because it shows how AI is categorizing the brand, even when it gets that wrong. And if AI believes something off about you, well… odds are part of your audience does too.

The share of voice info requires nuance. SparkToro shows up at 24% in ChatGPT and Perplexity, but just 12.5% in Gemini — where Semrush and Similarweb both rank higher. Our prospects might currently believe we all have the same offerings even though we don't.

The growth areas were plausible but generic: more enterprise features, more integrations, more automation. Useful? Sure. But you could advise that for most SaaS companies.

So here's what I'd do next:

1/ Find what's accurate.
What is AI already getting right? For us: audience research, credibility among marketers, ease of use and affordability, unique insights, and credible data sources.

2/ Find the misconceptions.
Where is AI putting us in the wrong category, comparing us to the wrong tools, or understating what we do?

3/ Turn the gaps into priorities.
That's a to-do list for website copy, content, PR, sales enablement, and category education.

Then run this analysis again in 1-2 months to see what moved.

HubSpot AEO Grader is a free way to see how your brand shows up across AI tools — what's resonating, and what you still need to clarify.

68% of Google searches now end without a click... to *anywhere.*

That finding comes from my co-author Rand Fishkin's latest research, and yes, it's a doozy. But before you spiral into "is SEO dead?" — don't.

Because the click was never the point. It was just the thing we could measure.

For years, we treated traffic like the scoreboard. More sessions meant the strategy was working. Less traffic meant something was broken.

But if Google, AI Overviews, Amazon, social feeds, and AI tools are increasingly filtering, ranking, summarizing, and recommending before a person ever lands on your site, then the job changes.

You don't just need to "get the click."

You need to become the brand people and machines look for by name.

I wrote up what I'd actually do about it:

- who's still winning Google's clicks, and why it's not just "better content"
- why AI-referred visitors may be fewer but more valuable
- why branded search, owned assets, and correlation dashboards matter more than traffic charts
- the 3 bets I'd make that survive every algorithm update

I regret to inform you that clicks probably aren't coming back. But you can build marketing that still works when they don't.

**Link:** [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/amandanat_everyone-got-told-to-10x-their-content-with-share-7469961075548041217-0MwT/](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/amandanat_everyone-got-told-to-10x-their-content-with-share-7469961075548041217-0MwT/)


**Format:** Text

Everyone got told to 10x their content with AI.

So now every other post reads like it was steeped with the same lukewarm teabag. Same structure. Same "It's not this. It's that." Same nothing.

They're no match for lived experience.

A large language model is a phenomenal line cook. Hand it a recipe that already exists online and it'll plate the dish faster and cleaner than you can. What it can't do is taste.

It wasn't on that customer call this morning. It didn't watch the feature flop and ruminate on why. It has no idea what it actually feels like to do the job — because it has never once done the job.

My friend Kaleigh Moore put it plainly on today's episode of Zero Click Marketing: the person who's hands-on with your product, talking to customers, running the experiments — that's a goldmine. Their point of view isn't published anywhere else. It's all firsthand.

Which means the most defensible content input you have is the people already on your payroll.
